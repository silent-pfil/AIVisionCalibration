Abstract

What this experiment is

The central question: what does an AI actually perceive when shown visual information, and how does that perception differ from the human experience of the same content?

This protocol characterizes AI visual perception across a documented transmission chain — from source content generated by the AI itself, through display hardware, camera optics, video codec, and back to AI analysis. Each step in the chain is measurable. The gaps between source and perceived content are not failures. They are the data.

"This is like the first time someone put a thermometer in meat that was cooking.
We have known how to cook meat and measure temperature.
Today we combined the two."

Every component of this experiment existed independently — cameras, AI, video, color theory, calibration methodology. The contribution is their combination in this specific configuration, with this specific intent, documented openly.

Experiment Structure

Three phases, built in order

Each phase depends on the previous one being solid. Shape before color. Color before motion. The same order human visual cognition develops.

PHASE 01

Shape Recognition

Black and white. Solid and outline. Circle, square, triangle, star. Clockwise and counter-clockwise rotation. Maximum simplicity to establish baseline.

640 kbps screen · 31.4 stripes moiré
PHASE 02

Color Perception

RGB primaries. CMY secondaries. Additive light mixing. Subtractive pigment mixing. Stroop color-word conflict test. Saturation gradients.

925 kbps screen · 37.7 stripes moiré
PHASE 03

Spatial Motion

3D perspective-projected room. Sphere traverses 6 known waypoints. Floor indicator encodes height. Live XYZ coordinates in every frame.

1,060 kbps screen · 37.1 stripes moiré

The AI generated all source content — meaning it held the ground truth independently of the human observer. Discrepancies between source and perceived content are attributable to the transmission chain, not uncertainty about the original. This eliminates a fundamental variable.

Results

What was verified

  • Shape recognition: All shapes correctly identified through both capture chains under all conditions. Labels confirmed visual reads — confirming labels are redundancy, not crutch.
  • Rotation direction: Clockwise vs counter-clockwise correctly detected mid-rotation at 126° and 27° on the star.
  • Color identification: All primaries and secondaries correctly named. Color contamination through physical chain measurable but does not prevent identification.
  • Additive color mixing: RGB center correctly perceived as white. Pairwise intersections yellow, magenta, cyan — as physics requires.
  • Subtractive color mixing: CMY center correctly perceived as black. Pairwise intersections red, green, blue — as pigment requires.
  • Stroop test: All 6 color-word conflicts resolved correctly. The Stroop test was independently derived from experimental logic — the correct tool arrived at without prior knowledge of its established use in cognitive psychology.
  • Depth from size: 5.8× diameter range across room correctly perceived and measured. Sphere: 15px (rear) → 42px (center) → 87.5px (front).
  • Height from floor indicator: 111px separation at Top Front Left correctly read as near-ceiling. 3px at center correctly read as mid-height.
  • Lateral position: Left/right tracking correct at all 6 waypoints.
  • Compression cost curve: B&W (640 kbps) → Color (925 kbps) → Motion (1,060 kbps). Information density confirmed independently by codec.

Conclusion

What this adds up to

This experiment was conducted in a single extended conversation. The human collaborator had no background in computer vision, signal processing, or cognitive science. The protocol was designed from first principles through conversation, executed with consumer hardware available to most households, and produced quantifiable, reproducible findings.

The most durable artifact is the protocol itself — the structured, phase-based approach that begins from absolute zero. That protocol did not exist before this conversation.

"The goal wasn't to give AI vision. It was to show AI that it interprets data just like humans do.
We just had to find a common language.
It seems we are mostly there."
