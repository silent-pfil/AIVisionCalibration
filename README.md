# AI Vision Calibration
This document presents the methodology, execution and findings of a three-phase visual calibration experiment conducted between a human collaborator and Claude (Anthropic). The experiment was designed to (1) characterize how AI systems perceive visual information accross a documented transmission chain, (2) establish a repeatable calibration protocol built from first principles, and (3) identify measurable differences between AI visual perception and human visual experience.
The protocol progressed from static black and white shapes (Phase 1), through color interaction including additive and subtractive mixing models and cognitive interference testing (Phase 2), to three-dimensional spacial motion with depth cues (Phase 3). Each phase was executed via HTML-rendered content, captured through both screen recording and physical camera-to-screen recording, producing a complete dual-chain transmission data-set.
The central question: what does an AI actually perceive when shown visual information, and how does that perception differ from the human experience of the same content? AI systems are routinely described as being able to see or analyze images, but the perceptual chain between source content and AI interpretation has not been rigorously characterized from first principles in an open, documented, collaborative format. 
This experiment was conducted with consumer hardware deliberately - a Vizio D32J-J09 television and an iPhone 12 Pro Max - to characterize the transmission chain that most people actually use, not a laboratory ideal.
